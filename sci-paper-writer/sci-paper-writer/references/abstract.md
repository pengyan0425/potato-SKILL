# Abstract — Guided Drafting Module

## Purpose
A self-contained summary that lets readers decide whether to read the full paper.
Typically 150-300 words (check journal requirements).

## Guiding Questions

Ask the user these questions before drafting:

### Context & Gap
1. What broader problem or knowledge gap does this study address?
2. Why is this gap important — what are the real-world or scientific consequences?

### Objective
3. What was the specific research question or hypothesis?
4. What did you aim to achieve that previous studies did not?

### Methods (brief)
5. What approach did you use? (experiment type, dataset, key technique)
6. What was the scope? (sample size, time period, system studied)

### Key Results
7. What are the 2-3 most important findings?
8. Any quantifiable results? (percentages, p-values, effect sizes)

### Significance
9. What is the main takeaway or implication?
10. How does this advance the field?

## Structure Template (IMRaD)

```
[Background] — 1-2 sentences on the problem and gap.
[Objective]  — 1 sentence: "Here, we [action verb] [what] to [purpose]."
[Methods]    — 1-2 sentences on approach and scope.
[Results]    — 2-3 sentences on key findings with numbers.
[Conclusion] — 1 sentence on significance/implication.
```

## Common Pitfalls
- Too much background (keep it to 1-2 sentences)
- Vague results ("results were good" → give numbers)
- Including citations in abstract (most journals prohibit this)
- Repeating information across sentences
- Exceeding word limit

## Action Verbs for Objective
"We present / propose / develop / investigate / examine / evaluate / analyze / demonstrate / design / construct / characterize / establish"

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Does it stand alone without the full paper?
- [ ] Are all key results included with specific numbers?
- [ ] Is the significance clear to a non-specialist?
- [ ] Within word limit?
- [ ] No citations?
