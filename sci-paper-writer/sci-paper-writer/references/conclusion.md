# Conclusion — Guided Drafting Module

## Purpose
Deliver the final take-home message. Leave the reader with a clear understanding
of what was done, what was found, and why it matters. Typically 1-2 paragraphs.

## Guiding Questions

### Core Message
1. If the reader remembers only ONE thing from this paper, what should it be?
2. What is the primary contribution stated in one sentence?

### Summary (brief)
3. What was the research question or objective?
4. What was the approach? (one phrase)
5. What are the key findings? (2-3 bullets, not detailed numbers)

### Significance
6. Why do these findings matter to the field?
7. What is the broader impact?
8. What has changed in our understanding because of this work?

### Forward Look
9. What should researchers do next?
10. What is the vision for where this work leads?

## Structure Template

```
Para 1: [Restate objective] + [Key findings summary] + [Primary conclusion]
Para 2 (optional): [Significance] + [Future outlook] + [Closing statement]
```

## Writing Patterns

### Stating the Conclusion
- "In conclusion, we have demonstrated that..."
- "This study establishes that..."
- "Our findings provide [evidence/support] for..."
- "Taken together, our results indicate that..."

### Highlighting Significance
- "These results have important implications for [field]."
- "This work represents a significant step toward [goal]."
- "Our approach offers a new framework for [application]."

### Forward-Looking Statements
- "Future work will focus on [direction]."
- "We anticipate that [prediction/expectation]."
- "This opens the door to [possibility]."

## Common Pitfalls
- Introducing new information not in Results/Discussion
- Being too vague ("more work is needed")
- Repeating the Abstract word-for-word
- Over-claiming or under-claiming
- Making it too long (this is NOT a summary of the whole paper)

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Primary contribution is stated clearly in one sentence
- [ ] No new data or claims introduced
- [ ] Connects back to the research question from Introduction
- [ ] Significance is articulated (so what?)
- [ ] Concise (1-2 paragraphs for most journals)
- [ ] Ends with a strong, memorable closing statement
