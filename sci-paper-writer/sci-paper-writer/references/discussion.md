# Discussion — Guided Drafting Module

## Purpose
Interpret results, connect to prior work, acknowledge limitations, and articulate significance.
This is where you make your results *mean* something.

## Guiding Questions

### Paragraph 1: Key Findings Summary
1. What are the 2-3 most important findings, stated concisely?
2. What is the single most important takeaway?

### Paragraphs 2-3: Interpretation & Context
3. How do these results compare to previous studies? (confirm, contradict, extend)
4. Why might these results have occurred? (mechanism, theory)
5. What do these findings mean for the broader field?
6. Are there unexpected findings that need explanation?

### Paragraph 4: Implications
7. What are the theoretical implications? (new understanding, revised model)
8. What are the practical implications? (applications, policy, clinical practice)
9. What new questions do these findings raise?

### Paragraph 5: Limitations
10. What are the main limitations? (sample size, generalizability, methodology)
11. How might these limitations affect the conclusions?
12. What would you do differently?

### Paragraph 6: Future Directions
13. What are the natural next steps?
14. What experiments or analyses would strengthen these findings?
15. How can the limitations be addressed?

## Structure Template

```
Para 1: [Key findings summary] — restate the main results without numbers
Para 2: [Comparison with prior work] — confirm/contradict/extend existing literature
Para 3: [Interpretation] — why these results, what they mean mechanistically
Para 4: [Implications] — theoretical and practical significance
Para 5: [Limitations] — honest assessment of weaknesses
Para 6: [Future directions] — what comes next
```

## Writing Patterns

### Summarizing Findings
- "Our results demonstrate that [finding], suggesting [implication]."
- "The most notable finding is that [result], which [significance]."
- "In this study, we found that [main result]."

### Comparing with Prior Work
- "These findings are consistent with [Author, Year], who reported..."
- "In contrast to [Author, Year], our results suggest..."
- "Our findings extend the work of [Author, Year] by showing..."
- "This discrepancy may be attributed to [difference in methods/population]."

### Interpreting Results
- "One possible explanation for this observation is..."
- "This result is likely due to [mechanism/reason]."
- "A potential mechanism underlying this effect is..."
- "This finding aligns with [theory/framework], which predicts..."

### Discussing Implications
- "These findings have important implications for [field/application]."
- "From a theoretical perspective, our results suggest..."
- "Practically, this work enables [application/advance]."
- "This opens new avenues for [research direction]."

### Acknowledging Limitations
- "Several limitations should be acknowledged."
- "First, [limitation], which may affect [aspect]."
- "Second, the use of [method/sample] limits generalizability to..."
- "Despite these limitations, [what IS supported]."

### Suggesting Future Work
- "Future studies should [action] to [goal]."
- "A natural next step would be to [investigation]."
- "To address [limitation], [proposed approach]."
- "We are currently investigating [ongoing work]."

## Common Pitfalls
- Simply restating results without interpretation
- Over-claiming beyond what the data supports
- Ignoring contradictory literature
- Limitations section that's too brief or superficial
- Future directions that are vague ("more research is needed")
- Not connecting back to the Introduction's gap

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Findings are interpreted, not just restated
- [ ] Comparison with prior literature is explicit (confirm/contradict/extend)
- [ ] Implications are articulated (theoretical + practical)
- [ ] Limitations are honest and specific
- [ ] Future directions are concrete and actionable
- [ ] Claims match the strength of the evidence (appropriate hedging)
- [ ] Discussion connects back to the Introduction's research question
